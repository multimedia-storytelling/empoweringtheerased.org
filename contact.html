Students started their journey to understand historical silences with simple questions.

“Why is it that certain groups of people's memories prevail in the history books more than others?” - Evan Clark

But through class discussions and lectures, students began to learn how and why silences emerge in the archives, leave gaps in the historical record and consequently depict an incomplete version of history.

“The narrative which used the sources and archives that included silenced peoples will lack the totality of the truth.” - Jake Winston

Silences, whether they are unintentional mistakes or purposeful missions by those in power to hide the truth, result in some people’s stories being erased from history.

“Many of the witnesses of history did not choose to stay quiet; they were forced to stay silent because they possess little power in the entire society.” - Helen Liu

The Nicaraguan Revolution

“In the 1960s, Nicaragua was facing a political, social, and economic crisis at the hands of the Somoza dictatorship. Poverty, malnutrition, and improper schooling and medicine were all factors that citizens were dealing with daily. At this time, women were supposed to be at home, taking care of the children, and doing all of the daily chores. Women faced the daily hardships of trying to make do with what little they could provide their families.” - Hannah Shiffert

In 1961, the Sandinista National Liberation Front was founded. Known as the FSLN in Nicaragua, the resistance group fought for the end of the Somoza dictatorship. Women were key contributors to the group because they could utilize their stereotypes of innocence and fragility to escape suspicion and gain access to complex communication networks. But when the FSLN overthrew the dictatorship and established a new government, only male members were appointed to leadership positions.

“Despite equally risking their lives to end the dictatorship, there is unequal remembrance of female FSLN fighters and lack of female representation in government settings.” - Hannah Shiffert
FSLN women try to make their mark today by pushing for reforms in Nicaragua’s health and education systems.  

“While females might not be given the deserved credit for all their hard work, they have left their mark on Nicaragua by improving basic human rights…Despite silencing their historical memories, these women have found other ways to make their voices heard through social reforms and the creation of women’s associations.” - Hannah Shiffert
